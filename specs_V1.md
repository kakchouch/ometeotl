
## Purpose

The project aims to design an abstract Python library for representing, generating, validating, serializing, and exploiting multi-actor, multi-space, and multi-metric systems in contexts such as simulation, symbolic AI, generative AI, strategic games, synthetic worlds, and structural analysis tools. The core must remain agnostic, extensible, and minimally teleological: it does not impose substantive objectives, but provides the primitives needed to represent finite, hierarchical, emergent actors that perceive a dynamic world imperfectly and act within shared spaces of existence.[^1_1]

## Principles

The theoretical framework is based on a few structural postulates: time is a fundamental dimension; every actor exists in a multi-dimensional space of existence; actions are abstract effect objects; metrics are heterogeneous; admissible intentions depend on capabilities; conflict can emerge from mere coexistence; and optimization can only be defined relative to an explicit interpretive framework. The library must therefore respect the following principles:[^1_1]

- Teleological neutrality: no concrete purpose must be imposed by the core.[^1_1]
- Extensibility: new spaces, metrics, actors, rules, and formats can be added without breaking structure.[^1_1]
- Multi-space: an actor or resource can exist in several spaces simultaneously.[^1_1]
- Hierarchy and emergence: an actor may contain other actors or emerge from perceived, real, or projected properties.[^1_1]
- Reality/perception dissociation: decisions depend on perception, not necessarily on ontological reality.[^1_1]
- Text interoperability: every object in the system must be exportable to a text format usable by AI systems.[^1_1]


## Theoretical Framework

### Axioms

The model is based on a minimal ontology in which objects, actors, resources, spaces of existence, and effects measured by heterogeneous metrics exist; time plays a fundamental role because all attributes of an actor, its resources, and its actions are time-indexed, and only the past part of the system is certain at the model level. Actors are finite, hierarchical, or emergent, embedded in several spaces of existence at once, and carry partial perceptions, constraints, resources, values, and intentions that are admissible only if they are compatible with their effective capabilities.[^1_1]

#### Summary list

- **Axiom 1 — Existence**: there are objects in the model; an object is any distinguishable and representable entity.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 2 — Temporality**: every object, actor, resource, and attribute exists in time; time is ordered, irreversible, and common to the model.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 3 — Spaces of existence**: every object exists in one or more spaces of existence, physical or abstract, whose validity depends on time.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 4 — Actor**: an actor is an object capable of perception, decision, and action.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 5 — Finitude**: every actor is finite in time, space, resources, and cognitive capacities.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 6 — Resources**: an actor’s resources are objects with their own spaces of existence, and may consist of objects, actors, or both.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 7 — Composition**: an actor may contain sub-actors with distinct attributes, constraints, and intentions.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 8 — Reality of the model**: the past of the model is ontologically certain at the system level.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 9 — Partial perception**: each actor has its own partial, biased, and potentially erroneous perception of the past, present, and future.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 10 — Reality/perception dissociation**: actors’ decisions depend on their perception, not on the ontological reality of the model.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 11 — Manipulability of perceptions**: perceptions can be modified by action, noise, forgetting, or informational transformation.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 12 — Objectives**: an actor may have objectives concerning its own space of existence, that of others, its resources, or its continuity.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 13 — Final and intermediate objectives**: final objectives may be stable and long-term; intermediate objectives emerge dynamically from the perceived state of the world, resources, and competition.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 14 — Capacity for conception**: an actor can only formulate or pursue objectives that it is capable of conceiving and sustaining in light of its resources, constraints, values, and horizon.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 15 — Coexistence**: two actors can interact only if they coexist in time and share at least one relevant space of existence.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 16 — Emergent conflict**: conflict can arise from mere coexistence, without prior hostile intent, if spaces, resources, or objectives overlap.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 17 — Harm**: harming an actor means reducing one or more dimensions of its space of existence toward null or critical values.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 18 — Emergence of actors**: actors can emerge from other actors, from perceived properties, or from projections, even without full formal existence.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 19 — Multiplicity of spaces**: spaces of existence may be physical, informational, symbolic, social, cognitive, legal, digital, or conceptual, and new spaces may emerge.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 20 — Structural isomorphism**: actions of radically different nature may be considered equivalent if they produce structurally analogous effects on relevant metrics.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 21 — Heterogeneous metrics**: effects are described by vectors of quantitative, qualitative, subjective, symbolic, or physical metrics, with no imposed common unit.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 22 — Interpretive framework**: any global optimum exists only relative to an explicit interpretive framework defining objectives, weights, values, and constraints.[^1_1]
- **Axiom 23 — Teleological neutrality**: the model does not presuppose any concrete goal; it provides a structure for representation, comparison, and evaluation.[^1_1]


## Game Theory Link

The link to game theory is natural, because the model already defines actors, shared spaces, limited resources, imperfect perceptions, admissible strategies, and utilities relative to an interpretive framework; game theory is therefore not an opportunistic addition, but a possible projection of the model onto an established academic framework of strategic optimization under interaction. More specifically, the axiomatics project the model toward dynamic, multi-actor, multi-space, and multi-metric games with imperfect information, contextual utilities, and sequential strategies rather than a simple static game.[^1_1]

#### Axiomatic translation

- **Actors → players**: each actor in the model can be projected as a player in a game.[^1_1]
- **Admissible actions → available strategies**: the set of actions compatible with resources, constraints, time, and space forms the set of admissible strategies.[^1_1]
- **World state → game state**: the multi-space configuration of actors, resources, and perceptions defines the current state of the game.[^1_1]
- **Partial perception → imperfect information**: games derived from the model are naturally games with incomplete or imperfect information.[^1_1]
- **Intermediate objectives → subgames / stages**: intermediate objectives structure strategic trajectories as successive subgames.[^1_1]
- **Heterogeneous metrics → multi-criteria utility functions**: utilities are not given a priori; they are derived from metrics via an interpretive framework.[^1_1]
- **Conflict through coexistence → strategic competition**: mere coexistence in a shared space is enough to generate a potential competitive game.[^1_1]
- **Limited resources → constraints and rivalries**: strategic rivalry can arise from overlap between resource spaces of existence, even without initial hostility.[^1_1]
- **Reduction of the adversary’s space of existence → strategic gain/loss**: harming an adversary amounts to reducing their useful space of existence, which translates naturally into game utilities.[^1_1]
- **Irreversible time → path-dependent dynamic games**: time irreversibility imposes a sequential, historical, and path-dependent reading of interactions.[^1_1]
- **Uncertain future → optimization under projection**: an actor does not optimize a certain future, but a transition from a known past to an anticipated future.[^1_1]
- **Internal hierarchy → nested games / internal coordination**: a composite actor can itself contain subgames of alignment, leadership, or coordination.[^1_1]


## Scope

The expected product is a modular, documented, and tested Python library organized around an abstract core and complementary modules. The base document legitimizes this scope by describing a framework for generating tools, representations, mappings, and structural comparisons, rather than a monolithic single-purpose tool.[^1_1]

The minimal functional scope includes:[^1_1]

- Representation of actors, sub-actors, and emergent actors.[^1_1]
- Representation of multiple spaces of existence and inter-space morphisms.[^1_1]
- Representation of abstract objects/actions and their effects.[^1_1]
- Representation of resources as finite entities with their own spaces of existence.[^1_1]
- Representation of quantitative, qualitative, subjective, or symbolic metrics.[^1_1]
- Representation of perceptions, beliefs, projections, and errors.[^1_1]
- Representation of final objectives, intermediate objectives, and interpretive frameworks.[^1_1]
- Structural, temporal, spatial, and cognitive admissibility validation.[^1_1]
- Export/import in JSON and YAML, with a dedicated LLM/SLM view.[^1_1]
- Contextual generation and symbolic reasoning interfaces.[^1_1]
- A game-theory interfacing layer.[^1_1]


## Business Requirements

The business model must make it possible to formalize an actor as an abstract entity characterized by its available objects/actions, spaces of existence, resources, internal constraints, temporality, values, and optionally its sub-actors; this structure is explicitly present in the axiom base discussed in the document. It must also allow the temporal variation of all attributes, coexistence as a condition for interaction, conflict as a potential reduction of another actor’s space of existence, and the emergence of projected or perceived actors without full ontological existence.[^1_1]

Essential business requirements:[^1_1]

- Every actor must have a stable identity, a type, a time horizon, and a dynamic space of existence.[^1_1]
- Every significant attribute must be time-indexable.[^1_1]
- An actor can pursue only intentions compatible with its capabilities, resources, constraints, values, and horizon.[^1_1]
- Final objectives must be decomposable into evolving intermediate objectives.[^1_1]
- Resources must be modeled as limited, consumable, transformable, or destructible entities.[^1_1]
- Actions must be comparable by structural effects rather than by their concrete nature.[^1_1]
- Composite actors must be able to carry unified, divergent, or minimal global intentions depending on their internal structure.[^1_1]


## Game Theory Requirements

Interfacing with game theory is a structural objective of the project, not a secondary addition, since the framework must converge toward multi-actor, multi-space, and multi-metric games with admissible strategies, abstract utility functions, and comparison of configurations under constraints. The document also specifies that the global optimum is never absolute but relative to an explicit interpretive framework defining objectives, weights, and ethical or structural constraints; this idea must be native to the API.[^1_1]

Specific requirements:[^1_1]

- Define a `Strategy` abstraction as a sequence or policy of admissible actions.[^1_1]
- Define a `UtilityFunction` abstraction operating on states, effects, and metrics.[^1_1]
- Support sequential, dynamic, multi-objective, and imperfect-information games.[^1_1]
- Support several regimes: cooperative, non-cooperative, hybrid, evolutionary.[^1_1]
- Allow computation or approximation of utilities relative to an interpretive framework.[^1_1]
- Allow comparison by partial dominance, robustness, or compromise fronts.[^1_1]
- Allow conversion from a modeled world to a game instance usable by an external solver.[^1_1]


## Export Requirements

The export constraint is central, because the library must serve as a representation interface for symbolic or generative AIs capable of building environments, actors, or strategies from context; the document fully justifies this orientation by presenting the model as a common language for representation and mapping. All objects in the core must therefore have a canonical, versioned, stable, reconstructible textual projection usable by automated processing.[^1_1]

Export requirements:[^1_1]

- All core business objects must implement a canonical serialization protocol.[^1_1]
- The reference canonical format must be JSON; YAML must be supported as a secondary view.[^1_1]
- Every export must contain at least `id`, `type`, `schema_version`, `attributes`, `relations`, `state`, `context`, `provenance`.[^1_1]
- Cycles and cross-references must be exported via identifier references, not infinite duplication.[^1_1]
- A dedicated `LLM/SLM` view must explicitly distinguish reality, perception, belief, hypothesis, and projection.[^1_1]
- Exports must be deterministic, diffable, and schema-validatable.[^1_1]
- Any schema break must trigger a version change.[^1_1]


## Validation Requirements

Validation is essential, because the library must filter objects generated by AI and prevent the introduction of objects inconsistent with the model axioms; this need follows directly from the admissibility principle and the separation between model structure and derived tools. Validation must be multiple: syntactic, structural, temporal, spatial, relational, cognitive, and strategic.[^1_1]

Validation requirements:[^1_1]

- Syntactic validation of JSON/YAML documents.[^1_1]
- Structural validation of types, fields, relations, and hierarchies.[^1_1]
- Temporal validation: no interaction outside temporal coexistence.[^1_1]
- Spatial/contextual validation: no interaction without a relevant shared space.[^1_1]
- Admissibility validation: no generation of objectives or strategies outside capabilities.[^1_1]
- Validation of perception/reality consistency: errors are allowed, but their epistemic status must be explicit.[^1_1]
- Minimum completeness validation for every instantiable object.[^1_1]


## Generation Requirements

The library must allow symbolic or generative AIs to build worlds, actors, perceptions, resources, or strategies from a context, then validate and instantiate those objects in the model. This orientation directly continues the idea in the document that the framework must serve as a meta-model for generating tools and representations, not as a prescriptive action system.[^1_1]

Generation requirements:[^1_1]

- Provide a `from_context` protocol for generable objects.[^1_1]
- Provide a pipeline `context -> text generation -> parsing -> validation -> instantiation`.[^1_1]
- Allow partial, incremental, or corrective generation.[^1_1]
- Allow generation of incomplete worlds with explicit uncertainty zones.[^1_1]
- Allow symbolic generation by rules and generative generation by structured prompt.[^1_1]
- Allow the library to propose repairs or diagnostics in case of validation failure.[^1_1]

## Acceptance Criteria

The product will be considered compliant if the core makes it possible to instantiate a small coherent world, serialize all its objects, reload them, validate their coherence, and then generate at least one admissible strategy and a game representation from that world. These criteria reflect the explicit purposes of the framework well: representation, comparison, generation, validation, and analytical interfacing.[^1_1]

Minimum criteria:[^1_1]

- Creation of a demo world with several actors, spaces, and resources.[^1_1]
- Full export in JSON and YAML.[^1_1]
- Re-import without essential structural loss.[^1_1]
- Successful validation of a coherent world and motivated rejection of an incoherent world.[^1_1]
- Generation of at least one actor and one strategy from a textual context.[^1_1]
- Construction of a simple game instance with relative utilities.[^1_1]
- Clear documentation of ontological statuses: real, perceived, hypothetical, emergent.[^1_1]
